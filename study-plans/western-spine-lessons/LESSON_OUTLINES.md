# Western Spine Lesson Outlines

## Purpose

These outlines turn the first Western source packets into teachable lessons without copying source text. Every outline is original course design and remains packet-gated.

## Packet Gate

A lesson is ready for original instruction when:

- its source packet exists
- no source text is copied into the outline
- the packet allows original lesson use
- the lesson includes cultural caveat, drill, assessment, revision path, and transfer case

A lesson is not ready for source excerpt use until the relevant packet is explicitly approved for excerpt use.

## Lesson 1: Euclidean Proof Craft

- Source packet: `source-packets/western/euclid-elements-casey.md`
- Source anchor: Euclid, `Elements`, definitions, postulates, common notions, construction, and proof sequence.
- Objective: Learner can build a proof-like explanation where each step follows from definitions, accepted premises, or prior steps.
- No copied source text: yes
- Cultural caveat: Euclidean proof trains discipline and clarity, but not every practical judgment can be forced into mathematical certainty.
- Core model: start with definitions, state what is granted, state what must be shown, then make every inferential step visible.
- Drill: Euclidean Step Test.
- Spoken practice: Explain each step aloud while a listener asks, "Why does that follow?"
- Assessment: Reconstruct a short real-world argument as a proof-like sequence and mark every missing premise.
- Revision path: Remove decorative claims, insert missing warrants, weaken overclaimed conclusions, and restate the proof in fewer steps.
- Transfer case: Turn a family, workplace, or policy disagreement into claims, premises, warrants, and conclusion.
- Mastery evidence: A proof reconstruction with no hidden premise that the learner cannot defend aloud.

## Lesson 2: Aristotelian Categories And Definitions

- Source packet: `source-packets/western/aristotle-categories-edghill.md`
- Source anchor: Aristotle, `Categories`, terms, predication, and classification.
- Objective: Learner can stabilize a disputed term and detect when an argument confuses kinds of claims.
- No copied source text: yes
- Cultural caveat: Aristotelian categories are a Western logical tool, not a universal taxonomy that should be imposed on every tradition without caveat.
- Core model: identify what kind of thing is being discussed before testing what is being claimed about it.
- Drill: Term Lock and Definition Tree.
- Spoken practice: A friendly questioner asks for examples, exclusions, and borderline cases.
- Assessment: Repair a confused public claim by defining the central term and naming the category error.
- Revision path: Add clearer examples, identify excluded meanings, separate moral/evaluative claims from descriptive claims, and retest borderline cases.
- Transfer case: Analyze words such as freedom, justice, harm, evidence, success, authority, or respect.
- Mastery evidence: A definition that remains stable under examples, counterexamples, and pressure questions.

## Lesson 3: Syllogism And Valid Inference

- Source packet: `source-packets/western/aristotle-prior-analytics-owen.md`
- Source anchor: Aristotle, `Prior Analytics`, syllogistic reasoning and valid inference.
- Objective: Learner can separate whether an argument is valid from whether its premises are true.
- No copied source text: yes
- Cultural caveat: Syllogistic form is a powerful discipline, but many real arguments use enthymeme, analogy, probability, testimony, or narrative.
- Core model: translate a persuasive claim into major premise, minor premise, and conclusion, then test form before testing truth.
- Drill: Syllogism Translation.
- Spoken practice: A skeptical listener challenges form first, then premise truth.
- Assessment: Translate a headline, slogan, or everyday claim into syllogistic form and identify the middle term.
- Revision path: Repair invalid form, expose hidden premises, weaken conclusions that outrun premises, and state premise uncertainty.
- Transfer case: Evaluate political slogans, sales claims, social media arguments, and workplace assumptions.
- Mastery evidence: Learner can say, "This follows if these premises are true," or "The conclusion does not follow," without confusing the two judgments.

## Lesson 4: Ethical Rhetoric And Audience

- Source packet: `source-packets/western/aristotle-rhetoric-cope-sandys.md`
- Source anchor: Aristotle, `Rhetoric`, ethos, pathos, logos, audience, and persuasion.
- Objective: Learner can adapt a sound argument to a real audience without hiding weak evidence or manipulating emotion.
- No copied source text: yes
- Cultural caveat: Rhetoric should not be taught as mere influence technique; it must remain under truthfulness, fairness, and audience responsibility.
- Core model: fit argument, order, tone, examples, and emotional appeal to the listener while preserving the claim's limits.
- Drill: Speech Compression and Ethos/Pathos/Logos Audit.
- Spoken practice: Present the same argument to a friendly audience, skeptical audience, and confused audience.
- Assessment: Create a short persuasive speech outline with evidence notes and manipulation risks named.
- Revision path: Remove overstatement, add audience-specific definitions, strengthen evidence, and cut emotional pressure that outruns the argument.
- Transfer case: Explain a moral or civic claim to people who disagree but are still reachable.
- Mastery evidence: A persuasive outline that remains accurate when compressed and expanded.

## Lesson 5: Socratic Questioning Under Pressure

- Source packet: `source-packets/western/plato-apology-jowett.md`
- Source anchor: Plato, `Apology`, public defense, questioning, accusation, and moral seriousness.
- Objective: Learner can ask ordered questions and defend a claim under accusation without becoming evasive or contemptuous.
- No copied source text: yes
- Cultural caveat: Socratic questioning can clarify, but it can also become humiliating if detached from humility and moral purpose.
- Core model: clarify the accusation, identify the claim, ask for the standard, answer the strongest point, and keep conduct disciplined.
- Drill: Question Ladder and Combative Opponent Reset.
- Spoken practice: A skeptical listener challenges motive, definition, and conclusion.
- Assessment: Conduct a structured dialogue where the learner clarifies a hostile accusation and answers without returning contempt.
- Revision path: Remove trap questions, strengthen the opponent's best claim, answer directly, and add a self-audit of tone and courage.
- Transfer case: Respond to a public accusation, family conflict, or online attack with disciplined questions and clear answers.
- Mastery evidence: Learner can defend a claim while keeping the other person's strongest concern visible.
