# Reasoning Drills

## Drill 1: Term Lock

1. Pick one disputed term.
2. Write a working definition.
3. Name the genus and distinguishing feature if possible.
4. Give two clear examples.
5. Give two borderline cases.
6. Give one thing the term does not mean.
7. Re-read the argument and mark every place the term shifts.

## Drill 2: Claim Stack

1. Split a paragraph into individual claims.
2. Label each claim as definition, observation, causal claim, value claim, prediction, inference, or conclusion.
3. Identify which claims need evidence.
4. Identify which claims are doing the most work.
5. Rewrite the argument with the claims in logical order.

## Drill 3: Euclidean Step Test

1. State the starting definitions or assumptions.
2. State the desired conclusion.
3. Write each step on a separate line.
4. For each step, ask: from what previous step does this follow?
5. Mark missing premises.
6. Remove decorative rhetoric and see whether the proof still stands.

## Drill 4: Syllogism Translation

1. Find a claim that sounds persuasive.
2. Translate it into major premise, minor premise, and conclusion.
3. Identify the middle term.
4. Test whether the conclusion follows.
5. If invalid, repair the form or weaken the conclusion.

## Drill 5: Steelman Ladder

1. State the opposing view as opponents usually say it.
2. Remove insults, slogans, and weak examples.
3. Name the strongest concern behind it.
4. State the view so fairly that a serious opponent would accept it.
5. Answer only after completing the fair version.

## Drill 6: Fallacy Repair

1. Name the suspected fallacy.
2. Quote or summarize the exact move.
3. Explain why it fails.
4. Rewrite the argument into the strongest valid or plausible version.
5. Decide whether the repaired argument is still persuasive.

## Drill 7: Question Ladder

Ask questions in this order:

1. What do you mean by the key term?
2. What claim are you making exactly?
3. What reason supports it?
4. What would count against it?
5. What standard are we both using?
6. What follows if you are right?
7. What follows if you are wrong?

## Drill 8: Speech Compression

1. Explain the argument in five minutes.
2. Explain it in one minute.
3. Explain it in one sentence.
4. Add back only what a real listener needs.
5. Check whether compression changed the claim.

## Drill 9: Combative Opponent Reset

When the other person attacks instead of argues:

1. Name the claim you are willing to answer.
2. Refuse the insult without returning it.
3. Ask for the standard of proof.
4. Answer the strongest claim, not the noise.
5. End the exchange if the person will not return to reasons.

## Drill 10: Self-Audit

After any argument, write:

- where I defined terms well
- where I shifted terms
- where I overstated evidence
- where I represented the other person fairly
- where I caricatured or dodged
- what I will practice before the next discussion

## Drill 11: Source-Move Extraction

1. Pick a source reference from the canon.
2. Identify one reasoning move the source trains.
3. Write the move in one sentence without copying source text.
4. Create a tiny modern example that uses the move.
5. Create a tiny modern example that abuses the move.
6. Turn the move into a repeatable five-minute drill.
7. Name the assessment that would prove the learner can use it.

## Drill 12: Objection Box

1. State your claim.
2. Write the three strongest objections against it.
3. For each objection, decide whether it attacks a term, premise, inference, evidence, priority, or consequence.
4. Answer the strongest objection first.
5. Name what you would concede if the objection is partly right.
6. Rewrite your claim with the concession included.

## Drill 13: Induction Ladder

1. State the general claim.
2. List the observations supporting it.
3. List rival explanations.
4. Name the strongest counterexample.
5. State what additional evidence would strengthen or weaken the claim.
6. Reduce confidence if the evidence is thin.
7. Explain the result without overstating certainty.

## Drill 14: Evidence And Incentive Map

1. Identify the speaker, institution, audience, and incentive.
2. Separate observation, interpretation, prediction, and recommendation.
3. Ask who benefits if the claim is accepted.
4. Ask what evidence is missing.
5. Ask what would change the conclusion.
6. Rewrite the claim with confidence level and uncertainty stated.

## Drill 15: Eristic Shield

Use this when someone is trying to win by pressure rather than reason:

1. Name the active tactic privately: insult, speed, ambiguity, false dilemma, demand flood, motive attack, or topic shift.
2. State the claim you will answer.
3. Ask for one premise or one standard at a time.
4. Refuse to defend claims you did not make.
5. Slow the pace until each move is visible.
6. Leave if the other person refuses reasons.

## Drill 16: Civic Judgment Case

1. State the proposed action or policy.
2. Name the goods it tries to protect.
3. Name the goods it risks damaging.
4. Identify incentives created by the proposal.
5. Identify who pays the cost and who receives the benefit.
6. State the strongest argument for and against it.
7. Give a limited judgment and name the uncertainty.

## Drill 17: Teach-Back

1. Pick one reasoning move.
2. Teach it in three minutes to another person or simulated learner.
3. Ask the learner to apply it to a fresh example.
4. Diagnose the first place the learner gets confused.
5. Revise your explanation.
6. Record the correction path.
7. Repeat until the learner can explain the move back to you.

## Debate And Persuasion Practice Lab

This lab turns the individual drills above into live practice for speaking, debate, disagreement, and persuasion. The goal is not to defeat another person. The goal is to make the issue clearer, stop word games, expose the active tactic without mind-reading motives, and help a listener revise from the inside when revision is possible.

### Non-Negotiable Standards

- Define the disputed term before arguing about the conclusion.
- Answer the strongest fair version of the claim, not the weakest version.
- Name observable moves, not hidden motives.
- Ask one question at a time when the other person floods the exchange.
- Concede true points quickly and visibly.
- Separate persuasion from manipulation: no humiliation, false certainty, hidden threats, or audience deception.
- End or pause exchanges that stop being reason-responsive.

### Pressure Ladder

Use each level until the learner can pass twice in a row before moving up.

| Level | Opponent Style | Learner Task | Passing Standard |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Friendly learner | Explain the claim and define terms. | Listener can repeat the terms and conclusion accurately. |
| 2 | Sincere skeptic | Answer honest objections. | Learner concedes limits and improves the claim without losing structure. |
| 3 | Mixed audience | Persuade people with different concerns. | Learner adapts examples without changing the core claim. |
| 4 | Proud expert | Handle status pressure and technical challenge. | Learner asks for standards and separates expertise from inference. |
| 5 | Combative opponent | Stay calm under interruption and insult. | Learner returns to one claim, one premise, and one standard. |
| 6 | Disingenuous performer | Identify tactic, audience effect, and exit condition. | Learner refuses the game while still serving honest listeners. |
| 7 | Public defense | Persuade a resistant audience while being recorded or observed. | Learner remains clear, charitable, concise, and revision-ready. |

### Disingenuous-Move Recognition

Do not accuse the other person of bad faith unless the setting requires a boundary decision. Instead, name the public move and redirect.

| Move | What It Looks Like | Response Script |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Term drift | A key word changes meaning after each answer. | "Let's hold one definition for two minutes. Which meaning are we testing?" |
| Motte-and-bailey | A bold claim retreats to a trivial claim when challenged. | "The modest claim is different from the original one. Which claim do you want defended?" |
| Demand flood | Many objections arrive before one can be answered. | "Pick the strongest one. I will answer that first." |
| Standard shift | The proof standard changes after evidence is supplied. | "What standard would settle this before we inspect more evidence?" |
| Topic escape | The issue changes whenever pressure rises. | "That may matter later. Are we finished with the first claim?" |
| Motive attack | The person attacks character instead of the inference. | "My motive is not the evidence. What premise do you reject?" |
| Loaded wording | A conclusion is smuggled into a term or question. | "I do not accept the wording yet. Let's define the loaded term neutrally." |
| Audience bait | The opponent performs for approval instead of truth. | "For anyone listening, here is the exact claim and the exact evidence on the table." |
| Impossible standard | Nothing could count as enough evidence. | "What possible observation would change your mind?" |
| False urgency | Speed is used to prevent careful thought. | "This is worth getting right. I am going to slow the pace." |

### Practice Session Format

1. Choose one claim and one audience.
2. Write the disputed terms and the standard of proof.
3. Assign roles: advocate, opponent, listener, and observer.
4. Run a four-minute exchange.
5. The observer records only visible moves: term shift, concession, premise, evidence, tactic, or repair.
6. Pause for a two-minute repair conference.
7. Run the exchange again with the same claim and a harder opponent style.
8. End with a self-audit: what changed in my claim, tone, evidence, and audience understanding?

### Persuasion-To-Self-Revision Drill

Use this when the objective is to help the other person change their own mind.

1. Name the value or concern the other person is protecting.
2. Ask for the strongest reason behind the claim.
3. Reflect the concern in language the person would recognize.
4. Find the hinge: the premise, standard, example, or consequence that would matter if reconsidered.
5. Ask a question that lets the person inspect the hinge without humiliation.
6. Offer a limited alternative, not a total surrender demand.
7. Give the person a dignified sentence for changing position: "A narrower version of my claim is stronger."
8. Stop pressing once the person begins genuine revision.

### Scenario Bank

Rotate scenarios so learners practice with different people, styles, stakes, and emotional temperatures.

| Scenario | Audience Or Opponent | Main Skill |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Family disagreement | Defensive relative who hears correction as disrespect. | Preserve relationship while stabilizing terms. |
| Classroom seminar | Brilliant but overconfident student. | Separate fluency from warrant. |
| Workplace decision | Manager wants speed and certainty. | State risk, evidence, and decision threshold. |
| Public debate | Opponent performs for applause. | Serve the audience without mirroring performance. |
| Online comment thread | Anonymous person shifts topics and standards. | Decide whether to clarify, redirect, or exit. |
| Civic forum | Mixed audience with different values. | Translate the argument across concerns. |
| Technical review | Expert challenges a weak inference. | Concede expertise while testing the conclusion. |
| Moral conflict | Listener feels accused or ashamed. | Persuade through dignity, distinction, and concrete next step. |
| Sales or fundraising pitch | Audience suspects manipulation. | Build trust through limits and transparent evidence. |
| Crisis rumor | People are anxious and moving fast. | Slow urgency without sounding indifferent. |

### Observer Scorecard

Score each item 0, 1, or 2.

- Terms remained stable.
- The claim was stated exactly.
- The learner answered the strongest objection.
- The learner identified tactics without mind-reading.
- The learner made one useful concession.
- The learner adapted to the listener without changing the truth claim.
- The learner gave the other person a path to self-revision.
- The learner knew when to end, pause, or continue.

Passing score: 12 or higher, with no zero in stable terms, fair objection handling, or rhetorical ethics.

## Change Facilitation Research Lab

Use this lab when the goal is to help another person see the need for change and take ownership of the next step. The learner is not acting as a therapist. The learner is practicing ethical reasoning, coaching, persuasion, and follow-through using public-safe summaries of change research.

### Research Spine

| Research Area | Practical Question | Practice Move |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Motivational interviewing | What reason for change can the person say in their own words? | Reflect the value, ask permission, evoke change talk, avoid arguing the person into a corner. |
| Readiness-to-change | Is the person unaware, considering, preparing, acting, maintaining, or recovering from relapse? | Match the prompt to readiness instead of pushing the wrong step. |
| Self-determination | Does the plan protect autonomy, competence, and relatedness? | Offer choices, build skill confidence, and preserve relationship. |
| Self-efficacy | Does the person believe the next action is possible? | Lower the first step until success is plausible and visible. |
| Implementation intentions | When, where, and after what cue will the next action happen? | Build an if-then plan with an obstacle plan. |
| Habit and environment design | What cue, friction, reward, or context keeps the old pattern alive? | Change the environment, reduce friction, and add a practice loop. |
| Cognitive dissonance and identity | What identity threat would make the person defend the old view? | Invite a smaller truthful commitment the person can own. |
| Attitude-change and inoculation | What objection will attack the new commitment later? | Rehearse the counterargument before pressure arrives. |
| Reactance | Where might persuasion feel like control? | Ask permission, preserve choice, and avoid public humiliation. |
| Social norms and diffusion | Who makes the change feel normal, honorable, or costly? | Identify trusted messengers, visible examples, and group incentives. |
| Adult learning and coaching | What practice, feedback, retrieval, and revision will make change durable? | Convert insight into a repeated coached exercise. |

### Change Conversation Sequence

1. Define the target change in concrete behavior, not vague character language.
2. Ask what the person already values that the change would protect.
3. Reflect both sides of ambivalence without sarcasm.
4. Ask for a 0 to 10 readiness rating and why it is not lower.
5. Identify the smallest honest next step the person is willing to own.
6. Build an if-then plan: "If situation X happens, I will do Y."
7. Name the likely obstacle, temptation, or social pressure.
8. Design the environment so the better action is easier.
9. Rehearse the future objection or relapse moment.
10. Agree on a follow-up check that preserves dignity.

### Readiness-Matched Prompts

| Readiness State | Do | Do Not |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Unaware | Ask what outcome they want and what the current pattern costs. | Demand immediate confession or apology. |
| Considering | Explore pros, cons, values, and discrepancy. | Overload with advice before they ask for help. |
| Preparing | Build a small plan, cue, support, and obstacle response. | Leave the plan as a vague intention. |
| Acting | Reinforce effort, troubleshoot friction, and collect evidence. | Treat one success as permanent mastery. |
| Maintaining | Protect routines, social support, and identity. | Remove practice just because early gains appear. |
| Relapsing | Repair without shame and extract the failure pattern. | Use relapse as proof that change was fake. |

### Observer Scorecard

Score each item 0, 1, or 2.

- The target change was concrete.
- The learner reflected the other person's values accurately.
- The learner identified readiness before giving advice.
- The learner preserved autonomy and dignity.
- The next step was small enough to attempt.
- The plan included cue, obstacle, and follow-up.
- The learner anticipated relapse without shame.
- The learner used persuasion ethically and avoided hidden pressure.

Passing score: 12 or higher, with no zero in autonomy, concrete next step, or ethical persuasion.
