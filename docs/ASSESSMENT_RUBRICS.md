# Assessment Rubrics

## Assessment Philosophy

Recall is insufficient. The course must prove that the learner can reason, communicate, and self-correct. Most high-stakes assessments require writing, speaking, or live defense.

## Core Rubric Dimensions

| Dimension | Excellent | Developing | Not Yet |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Definition discipline | Terms are clear, stable, and distinguished from neighboring terms. | Main terms are named but some shifts remain. | Terms are vague, shifting, or assumed. |
| Argument structure | Claims, premises, warrants, objections, and conclusions are visible. | Some structure is visible but important links are missing. | The argument is mostly impression or assertion. |
| Inference quality | Steps follow from premises or the strength of the inference is honestly limited. | Several steps are plausible but under-defended. | Conclusions outrun premises or contain non sequiturs. |
| Source handling | Source ideas are represented accurately with clear boundaries. | Source is relevant but oversimplified or partly unclear. | Source is misused, copied without boundary, or absent. |
| Objection handling | Strong objections are stated fairly and answered directly. | Objections are present but weak or partly unanswered. | Opposing views are caricatured or ignored. |
| Rhetorical ethics | Persuasion serves clarity and truth; no manipulation or contempt. | Tone is mostly fair but occasionally overreaches. | Persuasion relies on heat, distortion, or audience manipulation. |
| Spoken clarity | Explanation is ordered, audible, precise, and responsive. | Main idea is understandable but pacing or structure weakens it. | Listener cannot follow the argument reliably. |
| Transfer | Skill is applied to a fresh real-world context. | Transfer is attempted but shallow. | Skill remains isolated to the exercise. |
| Self-correction | Learner identifies specific errors and revises effectively. | Learner notices some errors but revision is partial. | Learner resists correction or repeats the same error. |

## Assessment Types

- Proof reconstruction: rebuild a proof-like argument from definitions and premises.
- Argument map: diagram a real argument and identify missing assumptions.
- Fallacy repair: turn a flawed argument into the strongest defensible version.
- Steelman and reply: present an opposing view fairly, then answer it.
- Dialogue performance: hold a structured conversation under pressure.
- Speech: give a clear persuasive talk with ethical rhetorical constraints.
- Capstone portfolio: collect proof, analysis, dialogue, speech, and self-audit evidence.

## Capstone Standard

The final portfolio must include:

- one Euclidean-style proof reconstruction
- one Aristotelian syllogism and validity analysis
- one demonstration or first-principles explanation
- one fallacy repair packet
- one written steelman and reply
- one recorded or transcribed dialogue
- one persuasive speech outline and performance rubric
- one self-audit naming recurring reasoning weaknesses and practice plan

## Grading Rule

A learner cannot pass the capstone through style alone. If the reasoning is weak, the speech fails. If the reasoning is sound but the learner cannot communicate it to another person, the performance is incomplete.
