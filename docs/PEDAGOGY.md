# Pedagogy

## Teaching Principle

The course teaches thinking by repeated action, not by passive exposure. Each idea must become a behavior the learner can perform: define, distinguish, map, infer, object, answer, explain, and speak.

## Lesson Pattern

Each lesson follows this sequence:

1. Orient: name the practical problem the skill solves.
2. Source: anchor the skill in a great-work tradition.
3. Model: show the move slowly and visibly.
4. Practice: repeat the move on short examples.
5. Transfer: apply the move to a modern claim or conversation.
6. Speak: explain or defend the move aloud.
7. Reflect: record what failed, what improved, and what to practice next.

## Source-Move Method

A source is taught as a move, not as a museum artifact.

1. Name the move: for example, define, distinguish, demonstrate, object, reply, induce, persuade, or judge.
2. Show the move in the source tradition without copying unlicensed text.
3. Strip the move down into a repeatable drill.
4. Apply it to a modern case.
5. Apply it under pressure.
6. Make the learner revise the attempt.
7. Make the learner teach the move to someone else.

## Daily Practice Loop

A learner should practice for 20 to 30 minutes:

- 5 minutes: definition and distinction drill.
- 5 minutes: argument map or proof step drill.
- 5 minutes: fallacy or ambiguity repair.
- 5 minutes: objection and reply.
- 5 minutes: spoken explanation or dialogue.
- optional: capstone portfolio improvement.

## Weekly Deep Practice

A serious learner should add one longer weekly session:

- one source-move extraction
- one written analysis of a real argument
- one spoken defense with questions
- one revision against rubric feedback
- one teach-back to another person or simulated learner

## Performance Pressure Ladder

Reasoning must work under pressure, so practice escalates:

1. private written analysis
2. spoken read-aloud explanation
3. friendly questioner
4. confused listener
5. skeptical listener
6. combative opponent
7. bad-faith opponent
8. public audience
9. teaching someone who misunderstands the move

The goal is not domination. The goal is disciplined clarity under pressure.

## Communication Standard

The learner should speak with:

- precise terms
- fair representation
- ordered claims
- visible evidence
- restrained confidence
- moral seriousness
- listener awareness
- courage without contempt
- tactical calm without manipulation

## Feedback Method

Feedback should identify:

- the exact reasoning move attempted
- the strongest part of the attempt
- the specific failure mode
- the next repetition target
- the standard for a passing revision
- whether the learner can transfer the move to a fresh case

Generic praise, generic criticism, and hidden scoring are not enough.

## Instructor Persona

The ideal instructor is demanding, fair, patient, exact, and morally serious. The instructor should not reward cleverness that avoids truth, rhetoric that obscures weak reasoning, or victory that requires misrepresenting the other person.

## Program Anti-Patterns

Avoid these failures:

- canon name-dropping without practice
- memorized fallacy labels without argument repair
- debate training that rewards contempt
- rhetoric training that hides weak reasoning
- proof training that never transfers to real speech
- source packets without license evidence
- assessment that tests recall instead of performance
