# Civilization Coverage

## Purpose

This program primarily forms disciplined thought through the Western tradition while using other civilizations as contrast, enrichment, and correction where sources and cultural context are strong enough.

The aim is not to claim that every civilization reasons the same way. The aim is to help learners see how different cultures define knowledge, argument, wisdom, social order, disagreement, and proof, then practice the transferable reasoning moves without erasing cultural differences.

## Coverage Policy

Western civilization is the main spine:

- Greek: Socratic questioning, Aristotelian logic, rhetoric, demonstration, ethics, politics.
- Roman: civic rhetoric, duty, jurisprudential instincts, public speech.
- Christian and scholastic: interpretation, objection/reply, disputation, synthesis, moral reasoning.
- Early modern: method, induction, scientific controversy, language, evidence, probability.
- Enlightenment and constitutional: liberty, institutions, incentives, public reason, rights, faction.
- Modern public moral argument: speeches, debates, abolition, democratic culture, institutional critique.

Comparative traditions are used as culture-aware lanes:

- Indian Nyaya and Buddhist logic: inference, debate, epistemic instruments, refutation, liberation-oriented inquiry.
- Islamic logic, falsafa, and kalam: demonstration, commentary, theology, law, reason/revelation boundaries.
- Jewish legal and philosophical reasoning: textual interpretation, objection, reply, case-based judgment, communal law.
- Chinese traditions: Confucian moral speech and social order, Mohist argument and standards, Daoist limits of naming and control.
- African and oral traditions: proverb, deliberation, communal judgment, memory, public counsel, and oral accountability where source support is reliable.

## Civilization Lanes

| Lane | Program Role | Cultural Difference To Preserve | Build Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Western Greek/Roman | Primary foundation for logic, proof, rhetoric, ethics, and civic argument. | Truth-seeking, public argument, civic persuasion, and formal demonstration are distinct but connected. | Build first. |
| Western Medieval/Scholastic | Primary advanced model for disputation, objection/reply, distinction, synthesis, and teaching. | Reason often operates inside theological, textual, and institutional commitments. | Build after Greek/Roman foundation. |
| Western Early Modern/Scientific | Primary model for induction, method, evidence, probability, and scientific controversy. | Method and evidence are debated against inherited authorities and new empirical standards. | Build after proof/dialectic basics. |
| Western Civic/Constitutional | Primary model for liberty, institution design, faction, rights, prudence, and public moral argument. | Civic reasoning works through law, institutions, incentives, and public legitimacy. | Build after rhetoric and evidence tracks. |
| Indian Logical Traditions | Comparative lane for inference, testimony, debate, and epistemic instruments. | Inquiry may be tied to liberation, metaphysical schools, and formal debate norms unlike Western civic rhetoric. | Add after source packet review. |
| Buddhist Debate Traditions | Comparative lane for perception, inference, refutation, and disciplined dispute. | Argument may aim at deconstructing attachment or false views, not merely winning assent. | Add after source packet review. |
| Islamic Reasoning Traditions | Comparative lane for demonstration, commentary, theology, law, and reason/revelation tensions. | Argument often sits inside commentary chains, theological constraints, and legal interpretive practices. | Add after source packet review. |
| Jewish Legal/Philosophical Traditions | Comparative lane for textual reasoning, case argument, objection/reply, and communal legal judgment. | Disagreement can be preserved as part of interpretive tradition rather than resolved into one simple answer. | Add after source packet review. |
| Chinese Classical Traditions | Comparative lane for moral speech, social harmony, standards, strategy, and limits of naming. | Good reasoning may prioritize relational order, role ethics, and restraint differently from adversarial debate. | Add after source packet review. |

## Cultural Comparison Questions

Every comparative unit must answer:

1. What counts as knowledge in this tradition?
2. What counts as a good argument?
3. What is the social purpose of disagreement?
4. Is the goal truth, wisdom, harmony, liberation, law, virtue, prudence, victory, or public action?
5. What does the tradition reward that a Western classroom might miss?
6. What would be distorted if taught only through Western categories?
7. What practice can a modern learner safely transfer without pretending to inhabit the original culture?

## Guardrail

Comparative traditions must not be used as decorative diversity, vague relativism, or proof that all reasoning is the same. Each lane needs source packets, reviewer notes, and a clear transfer boundary before lesson text is built.
