# Culture Difference Framework

## Purpose

This framework keeps the program culturally honest. It lets the curriculum compare reasoning traditions without flattening them into a single Western logic template or treating cultural difference as an excuse for weak reasoning.

## Primary Rule

Teach Western civilization as the main spine. Teach other traditions as serious comparative reasoning systems with their own aims, social settings, texts, authorities, and disagreement norms.

## Required Lens Fields

Every civilization or tradition lane must record:

- `civilization`: the broad cultural-historical setting.
- `tradition`: the reasoning tradition or source family.
- `knowledge_standard`: what counts as knowledge, warrant, authority, testimony, proof, or wisdom.
- `argument_goal`: the purpose of argument or inquiry.
- `disagreement_norm`: how disagreement is expected to work.
- `authority_structure`: text, teacher, law, community, observation, demonstration, revelation, office, or public reason.
- `transferable_moves`: practices a modern learner can safely train.
- `misread_risks`: likely distortions if taught without cultural context.
- `review_status`: whether source and cultural review is still needed.

## Culture Difference Rubric

| Dimension | Western Spine Default | Comparative Warning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Knowledge | Often separates demonstration, probability, testimony, and practical judgment. | Other traditions may integrate knowledge with liberation, virtue, law, harmony, or revelation. |
| Argument | Often values explicit premises, public critique, proof, and persuasion. | Some traditions treat direct confrontation as lower-status, incomplete, or socially dangerous. |
| Disagreement | Can be dialectical, adversarial, legal, civic, or scholastic. | Disagreement may be ritualized, teacher-guided, preserved in commentary, or subordinated to harmony. |
| Authority | Text, reason, observation, law, and public institutions often compete. | Authority may be lineage, scripture, oral memory, sage, community, or metaphysical school. |
| Speech | Clarity and persuasion are major goals. | Silence, indirection, aphorism, proverb, or role-appropriate speech may carry reasoning force. |
| Practice | Debate, proof, essay, speech, and analysis are common forms. | Practice may be commentary, memorization, oral counsel, meditation, legal case, or teacher correction. |

## Misread Controls

Before a comparative lesson can be treated as ready:

1. Identify the Western category being used to introduce it.
2. Name what the category reveals.
3. Name what the category hides.
4. Add one cultural caveat directly in the lesson.
5. Add one misuse warning.
6. Require reviewer sign-off or mark the lesson `needs_cultural_review`.

## Teaching Standard

A learner should leave a comparative unit able to say:

- how the tradition reasons in its own setting
- how it differs from the Western spine
- what transferable move is worth practicing
- what should not be borrowed superficially
- where their understanding is still limited

## Prohibited Patterns

Do not:

- call every tradition a version of Greek logic
- use a tradition as a mascot for a modern opinion
- quote or summarize without source-packet evidence
- treat culture as immunity from criticism
- treat Western categories as neutral when they are actually framing the comparison
- teach adversarial techniques without ethical constraints
